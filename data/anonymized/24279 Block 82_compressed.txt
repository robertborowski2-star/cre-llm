SUMMARY
Over the past decade, economic conditions within the Edmonton region have been
volatile. Following a period of significant economic growth, world oil prices fell
dramatically in late 2014, which led to recessionary conditions throughout 2015 and
2016. After a modest recovery in 2017 and 2018, the economy contracted in 2019,
primarily driven by issues within the oil and gas sector, including limited pipeline
capacity and rising price differentials between [COMPANY] and world
oil prices.
Between 2020 and 2023, the Edmonton and Alberta economies experienced a sharp
pandemic-driven contraction followed by an uneven recovery and stabilization. In 2020,
[PERSON] caused significant job losses, rising unemployment, and broad real estate
disruption, although the residential sector proved relatively resilient. Economic
conditions rebounded in 2021 amid reopening activity, improving energy prices, and a
labour market recovery, driving stronger residential sales, rising home prices, and
tightening industrial vacancy. Office and retail vacancies remained elevated due to
prolonged issues stemming from the pandemic. Growth slowed in 2022 due to inflation,
rising interest rates, geopolitical uncertainty, and supply chain disruptions, though
strong oil and gas prices supported employment gains and continued industrial
strength. Trends in the Edmonton real estate market through 2022 remained consistent
with 2021. In 2023, elevated interest rates and softer energy prices further moderated

economic growth; however, strong immigration sustained housing demand and
supported multi-family residential market strength, stabilized industrial performance
and improved retail conditions. While the industrial and retail sectors remained solid,
office vacancy remained elevated.
Economic growth strengthened in 2024 as inflation eased, interest rates declined
significantly, and oil prices and production improved, supported by a narrowing price
differential between Canadian and global benchmarks. Despite positive job creation,
rapid population growth expanded the labour force and contributed to higher
unemployment rates in Alberta and Edmonton. Edmonton’s residential housing starts
increased significantly and average sale prices continued to rise, driven by population
growth, lower borrowing costs, and relative housing affordability, while residential
vacancy rates increased modestly due to accelerated new supply. Office vacancy
trended downward but remained elevated, particularly in the downtown core, while
limited new construction and strong leasing activity contributed to further declines in
industrial vacancy rates to levels not seen since mid-2015. Retail vacancy remained
low, supported by strong tenant demand and constrained new supply amid elevated
construction costs.
In 2025, Alberta and Edmonton experienced moderate economic growth amid
continued global and domestic uncertainty. Early-year concerns regarding potential
[CITY] tariffs and trade policy volatility weighed on business and consumer confidence;
however, Alberta’s economy continued to outperform many other provinces due to the
resilience of energy exports and ongoing population inflows, albeit at a lower pace than
2024. Labour force participation continued to rise at a faster pace than job creation,
resulting in a slight increase in the Edmonton and provincial unemployment rate.
Edmonton’s residential real estate activity in 2025 was supported by sustained
interprovincial migration, declining interest rates and comparatively affordable housing.
Housing starts remained elevated, particularly in apartment projects, contributing to
improved rental availability and higher vacancy rates. Industrial market fundamentals
remained stable, with low vacancy rates and stabilizing rental rates. Office space
leasing volume was strong in 2025 and vacancy rates trended downward as in-office
work policies continued to gain momentum, particularly with public service employees.
Looking ahead to 2026, Alberta’s economic outlook remains positive but more
balanced, with growth expected to moderate as population inflows normalize and
inflation and interest rates stabilize. Rising oil production, expanding export capacity

Summary

2014, which also represented a high point for new construction in [CITY]. There was a
dramatic decline in activity during 2015 and 2016, which was also consistent with trends
in the Edmonton metropolitan area. As economic conditions began to recover in 2017,
[COMPANY] experienced an increase of approximately 25% in overall construction activity
compared to the prior year driven by the residential and industrial sectors. However,
increases in building activity experienced in 2017 were lost in 2018, with levels declining
back to 2016 levels. Except for a slight increase in 2019, building activity remained at or
near 2016 levels from 2018 to 2020.
Consistent with the impact experienced by most municipalities within the Region,
including [CITY] Edmonton, the Covid-19 pandemic negatively influenced growth in
[CITY] in 2020. However, the economy began to recover in 2021, resulting in an uptick
in residential permits during the year, and further growth occurred through 2022, with
permits returning to levels not seen since 2015. Notable year-over-year growth was
demonstrated by both commercial and industrial properties through 2022, however,
business activity slowed during 2023, primarily driven by the effects of inflation and
increased interest rates, and permits declined slightly in all sectors, with the exception
of institutional, which saw a major decline. However, most recently in 2024 there was a
substantial increase in building activity primarily driven by the residential sector.

administratively nor otherwise are rechargeable to the tenants.

CAPITALIZATION TECHNIQUES
The process of discounting income expectancies to a present worth estimate is referred
to as “capitalization”. This present worth estimate, the result of the capitalization
process, is the amount that a prudent, typically informed investor would be willing to pay
at a fixed point in time for the right to receive the income stream produced by a particular
property. Appraisal methodology has several techniques available to measure the
relationship between income and value.
The investment profile of the subject complex is such that it would be attractive primarily
to institutional type investors. A review of the investment criteria typically applied by
such investors’ highlights that a majority of properties are being acquired on the basis
of an analysis of capitalization rates in conjunction with cash flow yields. That is,
investors are acquiring properties based on capitalization rates that are in line with
alternate buying behavior within market, provided that this rate will allow the investor to
maintain an adequate cash flow yield. This in turn will form the basis for analyzing of
the subject property.
The basis of [COMPANY] involves the extracting of ratios between the
net operating income which a property was producing as at the date of sale to the market
selling price. This technique is one of the most basic forms of capitalization and is a
derivative of [COMPANY] or, [COMPANY]. Typically,
the resulting ratio which is extracted is referred to as a “current earnings ratio”.
Traditionally, capitalization rates have represented the relationship between the current
net operating income of a property and its market value. This relationship does not imply
that the current net earnings are expected to remain the same throughout the tenure of
ownership, for the fact of the matter is that they could very likely rise or fall in the same
way that gross earnings fluctuate with changing market conditions. What is significant
about current earnings ratios is that it is the current earnings that are being utilized as
the unit of comparison simply because they are known and can be estimated with
relative certainty. If the future performance of a property is expected to rise or fall below
the norm, the capitalization rate will be modified accordingly, and when applied to the
amount of current earnings, will provide an appropriate indication of value.
