2. Retrospective Value Opinion refers to an effective date prior to the date of the
report. The use of clear language and consistent terminology in a retrospective
report (i.e. past tense throughout) is necessary for the reader not to be misled and to
understand market conditions as of the retrospective effective date. Data subsequent
to the effective date may be considered as confirmation of trends evident at that date.
It is up to the appraiser to determine an appropriate cut-off date. In the absence of
such data, the effective date is the cut-off date.
3. Prospective Value Opinion refers to an effective date following the date of the
report; it is a forecast. The use of clear language and consistent terminology in a
prospective report (i.e. future tense throughout) is necessary for the reader not to be
misled and to understand market conditions as of the prospective effective date.
Prospective value opinions are intended to reflect the current perceptions of market
participants as to the future. These opinions should be judged on the market
support for the forecasts when made, not whether in hindsight they in fact occurred.

conditions from which the prospective value opinion was developed, and absolving
the appraiser from responsibility for unforeseeable events that alter market
conditions prior to the effective date.

effective date. In the update, any changes in the status of the subject, in market

The Income Approach was applied in the valuation of the subject property as it is an
income producing investment. This approach leading to a value estimate has its
economic basis in the present worth of future potential benefits that may result from the
ownership of the premises appraised. Basically, it is a process of capitalizing the net
income stream. The rate at which the income stream is capitalized should be consistent
with its quantity, quality and durability.
Different methods of varying complexity are available to an appraisal practitioner for
expressing the future benefits and converting the potential income stream into a single
capital expression of value. Two methods applied regularly by investors in valuing
investment grade properties such as the subject are the Direct or Overall Capitalization
method, and the Discounted Cash Flow ([COMPANY]) method. These methods, in essence,
simulate the thought processes that typical buyers would probably apply in determining
the value of an income producing real property asset.
Each of these income valuation techniques displays certain limitations. Because of the
assumption of a stabilized net operating income, the major limitation of the Direct
(Overall) Capitalization method is the failure to explicitly consider the impact of future
expected income from rental growth and capital gain on value. The [CITY] method
involves a detailed analysis of all components contributing to the current and future
expected income from a property, which overcomes in part, the limitations of [COMPANY] method. The major weakness of the [CITY] method, however, is the
sensitivity of the results to the accuracy of the many forecasted inputs that are required.
For the purpose of this report, only the DCF Method was employed for the subject

DISCOUNTED CASH FLOW METHOD
The [CITY] method forecasts a detailed net income flow pro-forma for a specified holding
period. It requires projections of net operating income and an estimation of a deemed
disposition price at the end of the anticipated holding period. The net, estimated, future
receivables, when discounted to a present value at an appropriate rate, provide an
indication of the property's value.
This analysis will consider the present value of the net operating income over a specific
investment horizon. The net operating income is discounted on an annual basis at the
selected discount rate, and the discounted net disposition value of the property is added
to derive a Present Value estimate for the property. Although the assumed disposition
takes place at the end of the final year of the investment horizon, the [CITY] method
capitalizes the net operating income of the first year beyond the investment horizon as
the basis for reversion value. The result is then discounted over the full investment
horizon at the selected rate.
Investment Horizon: The length of the income projection period was established at ten
(10) years, which is intended to reflect a typical, stabilized income stream.
