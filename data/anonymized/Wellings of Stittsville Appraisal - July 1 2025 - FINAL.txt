5.2 Market Rent Analysis
The term “market rent” is generally defined as being the most probable rent that a rental unit would command if exposed to the
open market for a reasonable period of time. The process of estimating the market rent presumes that the space in question is
vacant and available for lease. Since no two rental units are alike, the estimation of market rent must account for the specific
characteristics of each particular location, as well as the physical features and functional attributes of each space. In order to
estimate market rent for the subject property, recent leasing activity in the subject property and at comparable premises have
been reviewed.
5.2.1 Definition and Key Features of 55+ Communities
55+ communities residential developments designed specifically for adults aged 55 and older. Unlike assisted living facilities,
55+ communities focus on independent living with amenities that promote an active lifestyle. Advantages include maintenance-
free living, resort-style amenities (fitness centres, pools, clubhouses), robust social activities, and the opportunity to connect with
like-minded peers in a similar demographic.
These communities often feature accessible design elements like single-level floor plans, grab bars, and wheelchair
accessibility. While visitors and family members are welcome for temporary stays, there are usually restrictions on long-term
residency by those under 55. Monthly costs range from $2,000-$5,000+ depending on location and amenities, offering residents
freedom from home maintenance responsibilities while providing opportunities for an engaged, active lifestyle.

5.6.2 Capitalization Rate Rationale
As of the effective date, there remained underlying uncertainty in the economy, tariff war with the [CITY], world conflict, increasing
unemployment, higher cost of living, immigration issues and more. The central bank’s war on inflation saw its trendsetting
interest rate surge by 475 basis points since March 2022. However, [COMPANY] has been reducing in the overnight
rate since June 2024 from 5.00% to a target overnight rate of 2.75%, which has remained unchanged for the past three
announcements as of June 6, 2025.
Demand for multi-residential rental apartments remained robust despite economic challenges, driven by tightening occupancy,
reduced tenant exodus to homeownership, and upward pressure on market rents. However, rising interest rates and flattening
rent growth have led to stricter lending criteria and lower loan-to-value ratios, necessitating greater equity from buyers. As well,
supply has increased in the short term with a number of units delivered in the past year creating a more competitive
environment and protracting lease-up periods. This dynamic, is considered to be temporary and not indicative of a longer term
trend. Multi-family asset investors consider both current and potential future returns based on cash flows, replacement costs,
and comparable property values. Rental apartment building owners have performed well due to supply constraints in major
Canadian cities, with newer, amenity-rich apartments experiencing good demand.
Some transactions in this analysis were negotiated before the sharp interest rate increase and/or subsequent rate decrease.
The narrow capitalization rate range reflects a market with numerous buyers, resulting in competitive, multi-round bidding
processes. However, due to the speed in which rates rose, the lack of deal flow and several institutional owners sitting on the
sidelines, it is difficult to gauge to what extent yields decompressed. Many investors are taking a “wait and see” approach.
Buyers are still cautious, and the bid-ask spread remains wider.
The subject is a high-density project in a central location of Ottawa with a number of features that attracts rents at the upper end
of the market range. Given the surrounding built form, it is unlikely that the subject building will have any direct competition of a
similar scale in proximity. This increases the scarcity and opportunities for investors to have a presence in one of the most more
affluent neighbourhoods in Ottawa.
Based on the foregoing analysis, a capitalization rate ([CITY]) between 4.50% and 5.00% of 4.750% has been selected for the
subject.

precisely revealed terms, for which the specified property rights should sell after reasonable exposure in a
competitive market under all conditions requisite to a fair sale, with the buyer and the seller each acting
prudently, knowledgeably, and for self-interest, and assuming that neither is under undue duress.
Highest and Best “The reasonably probable use of a property, that is physically possible, legally permissible, financially
Use feasible and maximally productive, and that results in the highest value.”

criteria. The highest and best use must be:
 [PERSON]: The size, shape, terrain and soil conditions of a parcel of land affect its physical
utility and adaptability. The size, design and condition of an improved property may suggest that
rehabilitation, conversion, or demolition is in order.
 Legally Permissible: Depends on public restrictions such as zoning, building codes, historic
preservation regulations and environmental controls, as well as the private or contractual restrictions
found in deeds and long-term leases.
 [PERSON] Uses that should produce returns that exceed the income required to satisfy
operating expenses and debt service (interest and amortization).
 [PERSON]: Among financially feasible uses, the use that produces the highest price or value
consistent with the rate of return warranted by the market.

Bond yields are not moving in tandem with [COMPANY] policy rate
Since June [COMPANY] has lowered its benchmark Overnight Lending Rate from 5% to 3%. The Prime lending rate,
which serves as a benchmark for residential and construction lending, has moved in lockstep with [COMPANY],
now at 5.2%. Falling rates have been instrumental in stimulating the economy and labour markets since the Fall.
Yet bond yields – which are the benchmarks for most commercial real estate debt – have remained volatile, even spiking in
January. This volatility can mostly be tied to concerns about the inflationary impacts of a trade war with the [CITY], as well as high
levels of government and household debt.
Bond yield volatility over the past few months has added some friction to investment decisions, clouding the market’s optimistic
outlook last fall.
Feds cut immigration to ease shortages of critical services and housing
As surging population growth put pressure on critical services like housing and healthcare over the past few years, Immigration,
Refugees, and [COMPANY] ([COMPANY]) revised its immigration targets over the next three years to moderate the level of
population growth.
The [COMPANY] plans to cut its [COMPANY] (PR) immigration targets by 20% from [COMPANY] to [COMPANY] in 2025 and moderately
reduce PR targets in 2026 and 2027. They will also seek to limit [COMPANY] ([COMPANY]) – such as temporary workers
and students - to 5% of the total population by 2027. [COMPANY] currently make up about 7.3%, which implies a net reduction of
nearly 900,000 NPRs over the next 2 years.
This shift in policy will have important implications for commercial real estate. Immigrants have been a key demand driver

ECONOMIC OVERVIEW: ONTARIO
Ontario’s economy is one of the most vulnerable to the U.S.-
Canada trade war (Chart 1) because of its large manufacturing
sector. For this reason, 2025 Ontario real GDP growth forecast
has been downgraded by more than nation-wide. We would have
reduced our forecast even further had it not been for the
momentum Ontario’s economy was carrying heading into these
trade tensions. For instance, tariff front-running gave Ontario’s
exports a solid boost in January, supporting first quarter growth.
This dynamic should also help moderate the impacts of tariffs for
a few months.
Ontario’s steel industry has been slapped with a 25% tariff on it’s
U.S.-bound shipments. Its output should contract in response, as
it did during the last time the industry faced tariffs in 2018/19.
Because of the steel sector’s small share of GDP (less than 1%),
these levies won’t be enough to blow the entire economy off
course. More severe would be the imposition of steep tariffs on
automotive products, which are by far the largest Ontario export
to [COMPANY] Indeed, sizeable tariffs on autos (and other
manufactured products) could come in April. On the other side,
Canadian retaliatory tariffs on steel and aluminum will likely raise
costs in the manufacturing and construction industries.
[COMPANY] has been active in response to [CITY]
tariffs by (for example) banning [CITY] firms from Ontario public
sector procurement and eliminating alcohol purchases. They’ve also released a plan which targets (ac- cording to the

net new spending as the plan is not fully costed. Elements of the plan include a $5 billion Protect [PERSON] (financial
support programs for large-scale industrial sectors) and $10 billion through a 6-month deferral of provincial business taxes.
Ontario’s housing market has been exceedingly weak in re- cent
months (Chart 2) as economic uncertainty, abetted by winter
storms, chilled activity. We look for a subdued performance for
much of the year as uncertainty weighs. In particular,
supply/demand balances indicate that there is too little demand
chasing too much supply, with downside price pressure likely
over the next several months. However, 2026 prospects are
brighter given the combination of pent-up demand, dissipating
uncertainty, and a quarterly firming in hiring.

NATIONAL TRENDS IN MULTI-FAMILY RESIDENTIAL RENTAL MARKET
Investment volumes fall for 4th consecutive year: Multifamily investment sales reached $9b nationally, down 4% versus
2023 and down 14% against the previous five-year average. However, multifamily sales in Calgary and Edmonton are near
record levels as these cities continue to outperform demographically and economically (see Figure 21).
Strong year of rental deliveries is helping affordability: Nearly 90,000 new purpose-built rental units were delivered across
Canada in 2024, the highest level since the 1970s. This is giving renters more options, leading to rising vacancy and slowing
rental growth. According to CMHC, rental vacancy rose from 1.5% in 2023 to 2.2% nationally and was up across nearly every
Canadian city versus 2023 (see Figure 22).
Asking rents fall to 18-month low nationally: According to [COMPANY] and [COMPANY], the national average asking rent fell to
$2,100 in January, its lowest in 18 months. Rents have been declining for much of the past year in Vancouver, Toronto,
[PERSON], and Calgary but have been trending up in Ottawa and Edmonton, as well as other prairie markets like Winnipeg and
[LOCATION].
Immigration cuts are slowing demand: The federal government is imposing limits on net new immigration to Canada,
essentially freezing new [COMPANY] visas and moving current NPRs into the Permanent Residency pool for the
next two years. If enforced, this policy will lead to a net population loss nationally in 2025 and 2026. This is already impacting
rental markets across Canada, a key contributor to rising vacancy and slowing rental growth.
Construction is at multi-decade high but starts are slowing: While rental and condominium deliveries are at their highest
level since the 1970s, new home starts have fallen for two consecutive years, and they have fallen the most in Toronto. This will
likely lead to tighter market conditions toward the end of the decade once demand catches up with current new supply. New
home construction varies greatly by market. According to CMHC, Calgary, Edmonton, and [PERSON] saw significant increases in
housing starts, up 40-70% y-o-y, while Toronto, Vancouver, and Ottawa saw starts fell between 10-20%.
