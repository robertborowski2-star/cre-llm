3 Market Overviews
3.1 Economic Overview: Ontario
Ontario’s economy is one of the most vulnerable to the U.S.-
Canada trade war (Chart 1) because of its large manufacturing
sector. For this reason, 2025 Ontario real GDP growth forecast
has been downgraded by more than nation-wide. We would have
reduced our forecast even further had it not been for the
momentum Ontario’s economy was carrying heading into these
trade tensions. For instance, tariff front-running gave Ontario’s
exports a solid boost in January, supporting first quarter growth.
This dynamic should also help moderate the impacts of tariffs for
a few months.
Ontario’s steel industry has been slapped with a 25% tariff on it’s
U.S.-bound shipments. Its output should contract in response, as
it did during the last time the industry faced tariffs in 2018/19.
Because of the steel sector’s small share of GDP (less than 1%),
these levies won’t be enough to blow the entire economy off
course. More severe would be the imposition of steep tariffs on
automotive products, which are by far the largest Ontario export
to [COMPANY] Indeed, sizeable tariffs on autos (and other
manufactured products) could come in April. On the other side,
Canadian retaliatory tariffs on steel and aluminium will likely
raise costs in the manufacturing and construction industries.
[COMPANY] has been active in response to [CITY]
tariffs by (for example) banning [CITY] firms from Ontario public
sector procurement and eliminating alcohol purchases. They’ve also released a plan which targets (ac- cording to the
government) some $40 billion in new investments, loans, and other financial supports. However, it’s unclear how much of that is
net new spending as the plan is not fully costed. Elements of the plan include a $5 billion Protect [PERSON] (financial
support programs for large-scale industrial sectors) and $10 billion through a 6-month deferral of provincial business taxes.
Ontario’s housing market has been exceedingly weak in re- cent
months (Chart 2) as economic uncertainty, abetted by winter
storms, chilled activity. We look for a subdued performance for
much of the year as uncertainty weighs. In particular,
supply/demand balances indicate that there is too little demand
chasing too much supply, with downside price pressure likely
over the next several months. However, 2026 prospects are
brighter given the combination of pent-up demand, dissipating
uncertainty, and a quarterly firming in hiring.

3.2 Ontario Apartment Market Overview
3.2.1 Rent Control
1997 legislative changes to rent controls positively impacted investment demand and value, notably through vacancy decontrol
which permits a landlord to charge a market rent for a vacant unit. [COMPANY] encouraged apartment market
upgrades and institutional investment.
Since 2007, rental controls have tightened:
• 2007 Residential Tenancy Act: CPI-based increases, stricter capital expenditure rules, and potential rent reductions.
• 2012 Amendment: 2.50% cap on annual rent increases for existing tenants.
• 2017 Rental Fairness Act: Extended rent control to all private units, tightened above-guideline increase rules.
As of November 15, 2018, rent control exemption applies to newly-built or converted units, while maintaining control for existing
tenants.
The 2025 Guideline Increase is set at 2.50% and the 2026 Guideline Increase is set at 2.10%.

6.2.2 Definition and Key Features of 55+ Communities
55+ communities residential developments designed specifically for adults aged 55 and older. Unlike assisted living facilities,

accessibility. While visitors and family members are welcome for temporary stays, there are usually restrictions on long-term
residency by those under 55. Monthly costs range from $2,000-$5,000+ depending on location and amenities, offering tenants
in this cohort the benefit of maintenance-free living while providing opportunities for an engaged, active lifestyle.

reduced tenant exodus to homeownership, and upward pressure on market rents. However, rising interest rates and flattening
rent growth have led to stricter lending criteria and lower loan-to-value ratios, necessitating greater equity from buyers. As well,
supply has increased in the short term with a number of units delivered in the past year creating a more competitive
environment and protracting lease-up periods. This dynamic, is considered to be temporary and not indicative of a longer term
trend. Multi-family asset investors consider both current and potential future returns based on cash flows, replacement costs,
and comparable property values. Rental apartment building owners have performed well due to supply constraints in major
Canadian cities, with newer, amenity-rich apartments experiencing good demand.
Some transactions in this analysis were negotiated before the sharp interest rate increase and/or subsequent rate decrease.
