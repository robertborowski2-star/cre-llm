Canadian economic indicators flashed several warning signs in the last few months, as the impact of [CITY] tariffs factor into
the economy. Year-over-year growth in Gross Domestic Product (GDP) compressed by 110 basis points (bps), from 2.3% in
Q1 to 1.2% in Q2 2025. Quarter-over-quarter, GDP declined by 0.4%. Trade is a major factor in the economic weakness,
noting the 7.5% decline in export volumes from Q1 to Q2 2025. Exports declined in the sectors highly integrated with [CITY]
supply chains, namely auto (-24.7%) and machinery and equipment (-9.4%), but less than 10% of Canadian exports are
currently impacted by [CITY] tariffs, as they fall outside the scope of [COMPANY]. Nevertheless, manufacturing and investment
activity are declining and dragging down Canadian GDP, while domestic consumption remains positive, staving off steeper
declines in economic output.
The longer-term outlook on domestic consumption is challenged, however, as the unemployment rate rises, and consumer
confidence weakens. Given this backdrop, the [COMPANY] decision to cut the policy rate 25 bps on September 17, 2025, to 2.50%
was a timely lift to debt and equity markets. Other bright spots have come from the federal government, such as the
removal of retaliatory tariffs on most [CITY] goods, and direct investments for the industrial sector via infrastructure projects,
national defence, housing development, and targeted relief programs for tariff-impacted businesses.
