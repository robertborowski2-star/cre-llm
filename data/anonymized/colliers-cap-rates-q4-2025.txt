leases expire or tenants turn over.
Sectors
In the closely watched office sector, cap rates remain lower for most downtowns relative to the suburbs, although the rates are equal for some
markets such as Edmonton, Calgary, [COMPANY] and [PERSON]. This is despite vacancy rates that are persistently higher for downtowns,
a significant change from the pre-pandemic norm. However, rapid momentum in return-to-office is lifting the downtown leasing market,
especially in markets with large occupiers such as banks, government, and professional services. The end of a development cycle should also
stabilize the downtown market and mitigate some of the weakness in the leasing market. Leasing markets that are dependent on government
tenants ([CITY], Ottawa, Edmonton) are still weak and vacancy has not recovered as quickly as Toronto and other markets.
Industrial cap rates remain significantly lower than office, and largely unchanged throughout the year. Industrial rent growth – once close to
40% annually in some markets – has come back to earth. But after the initial scare of tariffs, where the impact seemed highly focused on
industrial users such as automotive and metals, the leasing market seems to have recovered, especially for smaller bay assets.
Multifamily remains the lowest cap rate asset, as a deep buyer pool, high replacement costs, and the possibility of CMHC financing driving
a strong investment appetite. Alternative assets such as self-storage and hotels continue to be in favor of investors, with sub-5% cap rates for
high-quality assets in Toronto and Vancouver.
Forecast
The outlook for 2026 is neutral, with most predictions in our survey indicating stable yields over Q1. Some weakness is expected in housing
in Ontario and Quebec, where rent declines are impacting underwriting and investment for multifamily assets. Conversely, some downward
pressure on yields is forecasted for in-demand assets such as grocery-anchored retail in major markets, where lack of supply, favorable lending,
and a desire for defensive assets should drive down yields.
